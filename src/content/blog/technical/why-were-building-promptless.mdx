"AI won't replace people. But people who use AI well will replace people who don't."

I first heard this framing from [Andrew Ng](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-mIjwN1o7nE)—who taught my machine learning class at Stanford (CS 229)—about two years ago. But the idea isn't his alone. Brynjolfsson and McAfee at MIT [wrote about it in HBR](https://hbr.org/podcast/2017/07/how-ai-is-already-changing-business) back in 2017, Karim Lakhani at Harvard Business School titled an [entire piece around it](https://hbr.org/2023/08/ai-wont-replace-humans-but-humans-with-ai-will-replace-humans-without-ai), and Garry Kasparov (my all-time favorite chess player) has been [saying a version of it](https://www.kasparov.com/the-real-threat-from-chatgpt-isnt-ai-its-centaurs-pcgamer-february-13-2023/) since his "centaur chess" days, that you should worry less about AI and more about the human next to you who's using it.

The framing varies but the point is the same. The question isn't whether AI takes your job. It's whether you learn to work with it before the person next to you does.

Everything we do at Promptless is about ensuring that as many people as possible are in the "people who use AI well" camp, rather than "people whose labor is replaced by those that use AI well".

## The actual problem

Here's the thing about AI in 2026: the gap between what it can do and what most people are doing with it is enormous. Frontier capabilities are extraordinary, and some pioneers (need a better word) are exploring those capabilities, but average adoption is still very basic. 

Of course, there have always been people who were better at using technology. If you knew how to use Google effectively in 2005 or were fluent in Excel, you could get more done. But those gaps were both bridgeable and much smaller in magnitude. 

This is where AI is meaningfully different—the gap between those that are connecting MCPs and evolving agent skills every day and the rest of us is insanely high (need better framing), and not everyone has the same capacity or desire to bridge that gap. At some point, a difference in degree becomes a difference in kind. This technological revolution is not like the others. 

You can see this gap play out in our own domain of documentation. The world's best technical writers are doing extraordinary things with AI right now. [Tom Johnson](https://idratherbewriting.com/) at Google has developed a whole "cyborg technical writer" thesis and co-hosts a [podcast with Fabrizio Ferri-Benedetti](https://idratherbewriting.com/blog/podcast-fabri-tom-sept-episode-1) at Elastic where they're working through what AI-augmented docs engineering actually looks like in practice. [Manny Silva](https://www.docsastests.com/) at Skyflow built [VerbaGPT](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/how-skyflow-creates-technical-content-in-days-using-amazon-bedrock/), a RAG-powered drafting tool to help his two-person docs team keep up with 60+ engineers, and presented on [AI agents redefining the docs pipeline](https://apithedocs.org/ai-25/manny-silva) at API the Docs. [CT Smith](https://docsgoblin.com/) has built custom Claude Code workflows with her entire style guide embedded, automating first-pass editing and content generation while keeping deterministic tooling underneath.

These people are incredible. They're exactly the "people who use AI well" that Andrew Ng was talking about. But they're also the exception. Most technical writers—and certainly most engineers and PMs who end up responsible for docs—aren't building custom AI pipelines. They're copy-pasting into ChatGPT and hoping for the best, or they're not using AI at all. The gap between what the best practitioners are doing and what everyone else is doing is massive, and it's growing.

## What actually works

And the dominant interface isn't helping. Most knowledge workers still interact with AI by typing questions into a chatbox—the same paradigm from 2022. Chat puts the entire burden on you to know what to ask, when to ask it, and how to use whatever comes back. That works for researchers and tinkerers. It fails for the 99% of knowledge workers who just need their work to get done better and faster. I wrote this in our original business plan in mid-2024 and it's more true now than ever.

AI that's embedded in the workflow. AI that understands context, triggers automatically, and produces useful output without anyone prompting it.

This is literally what we build. When a developer merges code that changes how a product works, Promptless automatically understands the change, figures out what docs need updating, drafts the update, and presents it for review. No one prompts it. No one logs into an app. It just happens, because it's wired into the systems teams already use.

Documentation is the beachhead. The real thing we're building is the pattern for how AI should work in professional settings: contextual, automatic, invisible until you need to review its output.

## We felt this

Before Promptless, I was VP of Product and Engineering at Bond, an embedded finance API platform that was [acquired by FIS in 2023](https://techcrunch.com/2023/06/09/fis-acquires-fintech-infrastructure-startup-bond). Bond's API documentation was broad—KYC, KYB, account opening, card issuing, ACH repayments, PCI compliance, transaction ledgers—and as the product evolved, the docs couldn't keep up. Changes would ripple across multiple guides and API references, and there was no good way to manage that in the development lifecycle.

We tried everything. In 2021, we hired a dedicated DevEx team with two technical writer consultants and a PM managing our API guides. They failed because they lacked context—context about how different parts of the product interact, the motivation behind changes, the specific things client developers were struggling with. The docs ended up full of mistakes, prioritizing lengthy prose and flashy graphics over the actual workflows developers needed. The overhaul required to fix it was so daunting it never got done.

We laid off the DevEx team. Then the Head of Product and I took over docs ourselves. Quality improved because we had the global context, but now the only people who understood how the product worked end-to-end were also the people whose time was most stretched thin. Our [changelog](https://docs.bond.tech/changelog) was almost entirely abandoned. Clients noticed.

This problem wasn't unique to Bond. Almost every API company struggles with it. The ones that do it well invest massively—Stripe's docs, canonically referenced as the gold standard, are managed by 20+ dedicated documentation engineers and technical writers. Good docs shouldn't require millions of dollars of dedicated annual budget.

## Why docs

People ask why we chose documentation. Seems unglamorous. But this problem is uniquely interesting and uniquely important.

The need for good technical docs is exploding, and AI itself is driving it from four directions at once. First, AI coding agents rely on public documentation to write code—bad docs now mean bad AI-generated code, at scale. Second, AI is making it easier for enterprises to build custom software rather than buy SaaS, which means more products exposing public APIs that need documentation. Third, AI is accelerating development velocity across the board, so docs need to keep pace with faster-moving codebases. Fourth, AI is bringing a wave of inexperienced engineers—or straight-up non-engineers—into API consumption, making documentation that caters to all skill levels more important than ever.

The very technology making documentation harder to maintain is also making it more critical. And most of the current "AI for docs" effort is superficial—language translation, style guide enforcement, search enhancement, chatbots for readers. Useful at the margins, but none of it addresses the core problem of actually keeping documentation accurate and up to date as the product changes.

Building an AI that does *that* well is genuinely hard. Documentation sits at the intersection of code, product knowledge, customer context, and communication. You need code understanding, an evolving model of how a product works, adaptation to each org's style, and writing that's actually good. Our engineers work on retrieval, code semantics, multi-agent orchestration, fine-tuning, visual grounding. It's some of the most technically challenging applied AI work happening right now.

That's why we attract strong engineers to what might look from the outside like "a docs company." From the inside, it's anything but.

## The broader point

Right now, the people benefiting most from AI are the people who were already technical. They have the skills to coax useful output from general-purpose tools. Everyone else is being left behind—not because the technology can't help them, but because the interface demands expertise they don't have.

Our thesis is that the fix isn't teaching everyone prompt engineering. It's building AI that doesn't require it. AI that shows up in your workflow and does the thing.

Every engineer at a Promptless customer who no longer manually updates docs after shipping code spends that time on higher-leverage work. Every product manager who was a bottleneck for doc reviews can focus on talking to customers. Every technical writer whose job was reactive scrambling can do proactive developer experience work. Whether a company has dedicated writers or not, Promptless changes the human role from writer to editor—from the person who has to produce the work to the person who reviews and refines it.

We're not replacing any of these people. We're giving them better tools so they can do better work.

## What we're really building

From the original business plan, something I think about often:

"What we're building is an evolving, constantly-updated, internal model of what a company's product does and how it should be used—including all the corner cases and customer use-cases, beyond the capacity of any single employee."

Documentation is the first output of that model. It won't be the last.

A company with a living AI understanding of their entire product—one that updates itself as the product changes—has a massive advantage. Not just for docs. For support, onboarding, sales enablement, internal knowledge management.

We started with docs because we felt the pain firsthand, and because it's concretely solvable today. But what we're building is much bigger.

## Urgency

The gap between AI-enabled workers and everyone else widens every month. Every new model release raises the ceiling. People already good at using AI get more productive. People who aren't fall further behind.

This isn't theoretical. Companies using Promptless ship documentation updates in hours instead of weeks. Quality is higher because the AI has more context than any single person. Developers are happier because the docs are accurate. Customers are happier because they can trust them.

Companies without something like this are falling behind, and they might not know it yet.

We're building Promptless because the biggest impact AI can have isn't replacing people—it's making the people who use it dramatically more capable. And the way to do that isn't chat boxes and prompt engineering. It's AI that's deeply embedded in the work itself.
