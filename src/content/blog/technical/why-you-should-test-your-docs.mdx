Good documentation is hard. Not because writing is hard (though it is), but
because the thinking behind it is hard. You have to understand the problems your
users face, figure out how to structure information so that someone with a
specific problem can find the right page, the right section, and get unstuck.
You have to think about information architecture, progressive disclosure, and
the right level of detail for each audience. That work takes a lot of effort
that is repaid in small increments as your customers, leads, and prospects find
the answers they need.

The tricky bit is that once you have done all that thinking and planning, you
end up with documentation that is tightly coupled to your product. The docs
describe specific screens, specific API responses, and specific command outputs.
That coupling is the point. Your docs are valuable precisely because they
reflect what the product actually does.

That coupling has a cost. Anyone on the team can change the product. A
developer fixes deficiencies in the API to make it more useful. A designer
moves a button. A backend change alters a response format. The product changes,
the docs do not, and now your carefully structured documentation is lying to
your users. All that time you spent thinking about how to guide people through
their problems is wasted because step 3 no longer matches what they see on
screen.

A lot of people chalk this up to a coordination problem between engineering and
technical writing, and they are right. Promptless goes a long way toward fixing
this by routing product and engineering changes to the people responsible for
docs. It watches pull requests, code changes, and team conversations, then flags
what documentation is likely to be impacted before those changes ship.

Engineers are experimenting with ways to ship changes faster and faster.
Technical writers are responsible for communicating exactly how users can solve
their problems with the product. Every time a writer documents a procedure, they
are making assumptions about how the product works. Without tooling, the only
way to check those assumptions is to manually verify each procedure, and that
does not scale.

Doc testing gives technical writers the ability to automatically verify those
assumptions. When something in the product changes and breaks the contract with
the documentation, tests fail. Writers do not have to spend their time manually
checking up on engineering. They can focus on understanding users and
structuring information while tests handle verification.
